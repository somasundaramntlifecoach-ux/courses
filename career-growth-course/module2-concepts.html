Module 2: The Promotion Equation

Page 1 of 3 · Concepts & Real-Life Examples
"I just need to work harder" is the most common — and most incomplete — theory of promotion. It isn't wrong, exactly; it's just radically insufficient on its own. This module introduces a simple equation that explains why hard work alone often doesn't translate into promotion, and gives you a concrete way to see exactly where your own readiness is breaking down.

The Equation

Promotion Readiness = Performance × Perception × Positioning. It's multiplicative, not additive — a near-zero in any single factor drags the entire result toward zero, no matter how strong the other two are. A 9/10 performer with a 2/10 in positioning is not a 9 — they're closer to a 2.

Performance

Are you actually delivering at, or above, the level you're asking to be promoted into? This is necessary, but on its own it is rarely sufficient — plenty of strong performers stay stuck for years.

Perception

Do the right people — not just your immediate manager, but the wider circle who influences the decision — accurately perceive your performance? This is where the visibility work from Module 3 pays off directly.

Positioning

Has the actual promotion conversation happened, with the right timing, the right framing, and ideally a senior advocate in the room when the decision is discussed?

The Myth of 'It'll Get Noticed Eventually'

Waiting to be noticed is a passive strategy dressed up as humility. Almost everyone who gets promoted — whether they'd admit it or not — has actively, if quietly, managed all three factors.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Suresh, Bank Operations

Suresh rated himself honestly: Performance 9, Perception 4, Positioning 3. Multiplied together (9 × 4 × 3 = 108 out of a possible 1000), his real promotion readiness was far lower than his performance score alone suggested — and it explained, for the first time, why his strong appraisals hadn't converted into a promotion.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Anand vs. Vikram, Same Team

Anand (Performance 7, Perception 8, Positioning 7 = 392) was promoted over Vikram (Performance 9, Perception 3, Positioning 2 = 54), despite Vikram's official appraisal scores being higher on paper. Vikram was furious until he saw the equation — his performance was excellent, but almost nobody senior perceived it accurately, and no positioning conversation had ever taken place.
