Module 7: Managing Upward

Page 1 of 3 · Concepts & Real-Life Examples
Your manager is often the single most influential person in whether or when you get promoted — yet many professionals communicate with their manager reactively, only when asked, instead of deliberately shaping that relationship. This module covers how to manage upward so your manager naturally becomes an active advocate rather than a passive bystander.

Understand Their Pressures

Your manager has their own targets, constraints, and anxieties. Framing your contributions in terms of their priorities — not just your own — makes your case far easier for them to champion upward.

Proactive, Not Reactive, Updates

Don't wait to be asked. Share progress and, just as importantly, blockers before they become questions your manager has to chase down or, worse, gets surprised by.

Ask Directly About Promotion

Many people never explicitly state they want to be promoted, quietly assuming it's obvious from their performance. It rarely is obvious. Say it plainly, and ask exactly what's required.

Managing Expectations Both Ways

Understand the realistic promotion timelines and cycles in your organisation, and make sure your manager understands your own timeline and expectations too.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Ramesh, Operations Executive

Ramesh assumed his manager already knew he wanted to become Team Lead. After two full years of silence on the topic, he finally said explicitly: 'I want to be considered for Team Lead in the next cycle — what would I specifically need to demonstrate?' His manager was genuinely surprised he hadn't raised it sooner, and gave him a clear six-month roadmap that same week.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Kavya, Customer Success Manager

Kavya started sending a short 'heads up' message the moment a client issue started looking risky, well before it became a full escalation. This single habit built her manager's trust that she managed problems proactively rather than needing to be chased for updates — trust that directly shaped how her manager talked about her to others.
