Module 1: Why You're Stuck

Page 1 of 3 · Concepts & Real-Life Examples
Most professionals assume a stalled career means they simply aren't good enough. That assumption is usually wrong, and it's an expensive one — it sends people down the path of endless upskilling when the real blocker was never a skill at all. Career plateaus almost always trace back to one (or more) of three specific gaps: a Skill Gap, a Visibility Gap, or a Positioning Gap. Getting the diagnosis right before you act is the single highest-leverage thing you can do in this program, because it determines where the next nine modules should focus your energy.

The Skill Gap

You genuinely haven't yet built the capability the next role requires. This is real, but it is also the least common reason for a plateau — and ironically the one people assume most often, because it's the most comfortable explanation. 'I need to learn more' feels safer than 'I need to be seen differently.'

The Visibility Gap

You have the capability, but the people who influence promotion decisions don't have accurate information about your impact. Work that isn't seen might as well not exist for promotion purposes — however unfair that feels.

The Positioning Gap

You're capable and reasonably visible, but nobody has had the direct, explicit conversation about your promotion, or you lack a senior person advocating for you in rooms you're not in. This gap is often invisible to the person experiencing it.

Why Most People Misdiagnose Themselves

Because a skill gap is the least threatening explanation, people default to it even when their real problem is visibility or positioning. Reversing this default is most of the work of this module.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Rajesh, Senior Software Engineer, Chennai

Rajesh had excellent technical ratings for four straight years but was passed over twice for Team Lead. When he finally asked his skip-level manager directly what was missing, he learned something surprising: leadership had no idea he wanted to lead. He had always let his own manager present updates to senior stakeholders and had never once said, out loud, that he wanted the role. His gap wasn't skill — it was visibility and positioning, entirely self-inflicted by staying quiet.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Meena, HR Manager, Bengaluru

Meena assumed she needed an MBA before she could credibly move to Head of HR, and spent a year and considerable money pursuing one. When she finally shadowed the current Head of HR for a week as part of a stretch assignment, she realised the real gap was stakeholder management with the leadership team in high-pressure moments — a skill no MBA program was going to teach her directly.
