Module 6: Networking & Finding a Sponsor

Page 2 of 3 · Practical Guidance & Tips

Step-by-Step Guidance

  1. List 3 senior people, inside or outside your immediate department, who could plausibly become a sponsor.
  2. For each, identify one genuine, low-pressure way to start or deepen the relationship (a specific question, a shared project, a taskforce).
  3. Take the first concrete action with at least one of them this month.
  4. Look for ways to add value to that person's own goals, not just ask for their help.
  5. Revisit this list every quarter — sponsor relationships take sustained, not one-off, effort.

DO

  • Approach potential sponsors with genuine curiosity about their work, not an obvious agenda.
  • Volunteer for visible cross-functional work that naturally puts you in front of potential sponsors.
  • Follow through reliably on anything a potential sponsor sees you commit to — this is what builds trust.

DON'T

  • Don't treat networking as purely transactional or ask for help before any real relationship exists.
  • Don't assume your mentor automatically functions as your sponsor — they're often not the same person.
  • Don't wait for a formal opportunity to build the relationship; start with a specific, small first step.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing having a mentor with having a sponsor and assuming you're covered.
  • Only reaching out to senior people when you need something specific from them.
  • Never following up after an initial introduction, letting the relationship go cold.
SAMPLE SCRIPT

“I've really admired how your team approached [specific project] — would you be open to a short call sometime so I could understand your thinking on it?”
