Time & Priority Management for Leaders

Learning Objective

Manage your time and priorities as a leader, distinguishing urgent work from truly important leadership activities.

The Eisenhower Matrix for Leaders

Urgent & Important (do now), Important not Urgent (schedule — this is where real leadership work lives: coaching, planning, relationship-building), Urgent not Important (delegate), Neither (eliminate). Most leaders spend too much time in 'Urgent' and starve the 'Important not Urgent' quadrant.

Protecting Time for Leadership Work

Coaching conversations, strategic thinking, and relationship-building rarely feel urgent on any given day, so they get pushed indefinitely unless deliberately scheduled. Leaders who block calendar time for this see measurably stronger team outcomes over a quarter.

The Cost of Constant Availability

Being interruptible at all times fragments deep thinking and signals to the team that reactive fire-fighting is the norm. Setting reasonable response-time expectations (not silence, but not instant either) models healthier work patterns for the whole team.

Saying No as a Leadership Skill

Every 'yes' to a low-priority request is a 'no' to something more important, whether you say it explicitly or not. Leaders who can decline (or negotiate scope/timeline) respectfully protect both their own capacity and their team's focus.
