Conflict Resolution & Difficult Conversations

ContentReal-Life ExamplesMetaphorWorksheetAction Plan
Learning Objective

Handle disagreement and difficult conversations constructively, without avoidance or escalation.

Why Leaders Avoid Difficult Conversations

Fear of damaging the relationship, fear of an emotional reaction, or simply not knowing how to start. But avoided conflict doesn't disappear — it resurfaces as passive resistance, gossip, or disengagement, usually worse than the original issue.

Separating Position from Interest

In most conflicts, people state a position ('I want X') that hides an underlying interest ('I need to feel respected' or 'I need predictability'). Asking 'what matters to you about this?' surfaces the real issue faster than debating positions.

A Structure for Difficult Conversations

1) State your intention ("I want to work through this together"). 2) Describe the specific behaviour and impact, not character. 3) Invite their perspective and actually listen. 4) Co-create a way forward. 5) Agree on a follow-up point.

Managing Your Own Defensiveness

When receiving pushback, resist the urge to immediately justify or counter-argue. A brief acknowledgment ('I hear that this landed badly, tell me more') before responding keeps the conversation productive rather than combative.
